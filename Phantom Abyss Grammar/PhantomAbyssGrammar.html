Glyph Order-

The basic glyph order is right to left, with the primary glyph on the left and its modifiers on the right.

Strings of glyphs can get fairly long so be careful. Read from the rightmost end to the leftmost end.

“The oldest surviving descendant”

There are three exceptions to this rule: Compact verb structures, numbers and glyph #133 (but more on these later)

Verbs - Word Order -

The basic word order is Subject Verb Object, similar to English.

Verbs - Auxiliaries -

Verbs can utilize one of two auxiliaries: “Can” and “Should”. Similar to tenses, these auxiliaries are marked on

  • “Can” Auxiliary-

The “can” auxiliary is used to express potentiality; this includes expressions such as “to be able” and “if/then” statements. The glyphs to express these are the past potential, the present potential, and the future potential.

Using the potential auxiliary to express “to be able to” is easy; Simply replace  with one of the potentiality marking glyphs above.

 

   “A god can create life”    

Using the potential auxiliary to express “if/then” statements has four requirements.

1: The future potentialmust be used in the “if” clause. 

2: The “if” clause and the “then” clause must be separated by .

3: A subject must precede the verb in both clauses.

4: The “then” clause must be in the future tense

“If you have the relic, then you will become immortal”

  • “Should” Auxiliary-

The “should” auxiliary is used to express an expected outcome. The glyphs used to express this are the expected past, the expected present, and the expected future.  

“The king should have protected the kingdom”

Verbs - Adverbs -

The adverb of the verb has the same structure discussed in Glyph Order. That is, they are found to the right of the verbal glyph and are read right to left. There are four types of verbal adverbs: Manner, time, place, and recipient.

  • Manner Adverbs -

A manner adverb tells how the verb is performed. This includes negating the verb

“A king should not fearfully (with fear) rule”

  • Time Adverbs -

A time adverb tells when or for how long the verb is performed.

 

 “A child survived for a few years”    

     

“A child is currently surviving”

  • Recipient Adverbs -

A recipient adverbs tells at whom a verb was done. This is not a direct object, but rather an indirect object. Examples of this would be “give something to someone” or “tell something to someone.”

“The child told the truth to the king”

  • Place Adverbs -

A place adverb tells where a verb happens.

“Citizens live inside the town”

Remember to keep in mind basic glyph order when interpreting. Directional glyphs such as “in”can have various interpretations depending on its position relative to the verb and place-glyph, as well as to the tense particles.

It is important to note that a verb can have one, some, or even all types of verbal adverbs modifying it. The common order in which adverbs are listed (from left to right) is: Manner -> recipient -> place -> time -> negative marker. Note that this is the order in which these concepts can be found in The Ancient Language, and NOT in English. Be sure to re-order them for a natural translation.

“The child did not happily give the king money in the temple for a year”

Conjunctions-

A conjunction is a word used to combine two separate sentences into one. There are two conjunctions available for this purpose: “and”, and “but”. The “and”  conjunction is used to combine two separate sentences while the “but” is used to contrast two separate sentences.
